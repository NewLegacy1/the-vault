# Powell's rejection-block entry model — from his own free YouTube transcripts

All quotes below are verbatim (auto-caption) extracts from transcripts pulled 2026-07-17 from the "Powell trades" channel (`@Powelltrades`). Raw transcript files with timestamps: `vault-app/data/powell-transcripts/`. Timestamps cited as [m:ss] within each video.

## 1. What a rejection block is (his definition, not ICT's)

From **"The PD-array that makes me 6 figures per month"** (a3LzCUZU5ko) — his self-described "official video on rejection blocks":

> "a rejection block, as I'm sure a lot of you guys know by now, is a wick. But it's not just any wick... for a rejection block to be valid, we need to have some confirmation in the form of a candle close. So, if I'm bearish, I can't just use an up closed candle with a wick on top of it because that's not giving me any confirmation... I want to see a fat wick like this and then I want to see a bearish candle close." [~0:57–1:50]

He explicitly rejects the ICT-orthodox "last up-close candle" definition — **"How to structure a Rejection block entry"** (AGmRZ9Te9NY):

> "Technically, it's not a rejection block until this candle closes, right? This is a bearish candle... To me, it makes sense that this is a rejection block when the candle closes bearish because that's going to give me some actual bearish confirmation... ICT might teach it with it being the last up close candle. I don't really care." [~2:56–3:33]
> "I don't give a s— how ICT teaches rejection blocks. I'm not ICT. I don't trade like ICT. I trade his concepts." [~0:46–1:06]

**Two-tier quality criteria** (a3LzCUZU5ko):

> "We swept liquidity and we had a bearish candle close. You can say that that's good enough. Your win rate might not be the best, but technically if you want to simplify it to that degree, you can." [~0:44–0:49 region]

Tier 2 — sweep **into a level**:

> "we have a level in the form of an order block right above it. Now it doesn't have to be an order block. It can be... a fair value gap... a key open... a fib level. It could be a opening gap. It could be anything, any PDA. As long as we have liquidity below, we have those two confluences stacked together, we can actually go and make some killer rejection blocks." [~0:54–1:03 region]

So the minimum spec is: **liquidity sweep + validating close**; the A-grade spec is **liquidity sweep INTO a PDA + validating close**.

## 2. Exact entry placement

The canonical statement, from **"The setup that made me 60k in February"** (tNyT7tHOmGI):

> "you put your limit at the beginning of the rejection block, right? The rejection block gets made and the very next candle taps into it. You put your entry there, stop loss below the rejection block." [6:14–6:28]

"Beginning of the rejection block" = the wick start / candle open side (most premium/discount edge of the wick, nearest to price). Confirmed in the live 10 AM trade in the same video:

> "I put my entry at the rejection block, the start of the rejection block again, so it's at 485, I believe." [16:52–16:56]

When the RB is too big, use internal fractions — **"Stop getting manipulated"** (WEeXKMzaJjY), on a ~40-pt 5m RB:

> "you can use the CE, you can use the beginning of them... I just gauge the risk. This one is 15 points of risk... and 50 points of target. So risk-to-reward not that great. One to three. I would probably look to use the CE on this one... 10-point stop, 5.7 RR, way better." [~8:06–8:27]
> "you could have used the 25% of the rejection block which does end up playing out for a 5.6... or the CE which is also really good but it would have gotten edged and you would not have gotten in the trade. So it's kind of up to you... is it worse to get edged and not get in the trade or is it worse to take a low-risk-reward trade." [~11:05–11:36]

Why RB over IFVG (tNyT7tHOmGI):

> "instead of waiting for a inverse fair value gap, you just use a rejection block instead. Your win rate is going to be lower than a IFVG, but... your risk reward is going to weigh up for it like tenfold." [5:53–6:08]

## 3. Stop placement — extreme is the *ideal*, not the only rule

Baseline (a3LzCUZU5ko):

> "We're using this wick as an entry trigger. And we're having our stop loss right above it." [~1:12 region]
> "Have your stop loss at the high or low of the rejection block depending on if you're bullish or bearish. And then you just aim for some internal structure or a static RR." [~1:48 region]

But in his own recorded 10 AM trade he covered the PD-array midpoint instead of the full wick (tNyT7tHOmGI):

> "Then I had a 15-point stop just to cover the the midpoint, right? This line in the PD array. I want my stop loss to cover that, because that is going to defend me... That is a sensitive point usually within every single PD array. ... Now, ideally, your stop should be at the end of the rejection block to cover the entire low[, in] case there's going to be SMTs... but I liked this better." [17:01–17:57]
> "this was a 1:8. If I did have my stop loss at the low, 1:5 still very acceptable, but I like this a lot more." [17:58–18:14]

Alternative anchor — beyond the **swept internal high/low** of the manipulation leg (AGmRZ9Te9NY):

> "we put in another rejection block after we actually sweep out this high. I was like, 'Cool, that's a good manipulation move.' So, I just put a limit right at that level... And then I did put my stop loss above this [the swept high] in that case because we already manipulated. If we go back up above this high again and I'm in that trade, not really something I want to see play out." [6:14–7:00]

Stop-size evolution: "I used to do like two to 10 point stops... 10 point was like the absolute max. Now, I don't really care. I want to be on side with the market" (AGmRZ9Te9NY, ~5:40–6:20 — he now sizes 10–15 pts and prioritizes win rate over sniper stops). In "tick precision entries" (Y-oqSZmNo4U): "I could get away with a 10 to 15 point stop... just like let price breathe a little bit because we do have the most volatile session." The live-execution video (4COROwkO3DI) used a 10-pt initial stop for 1:7.

## 4. Targets

No fixed R multiple and no point cap — targets are structural:

- a3LzCUZU5ko: "all of this, if we go to the one minute time frame, is lower resistance liquidity. So, you can aim for the very low of the low resistance liquidity range or you can aim for internal structure like imbalances... or you can aim for a fib."
- tNyT7tHOmGI (live trade): "I was targeting 605 right there. It's an unfilled gap. It's a old low right there. And um yeah. So, this was a 1:8." [17:58–18:07]
- Y-oqSZmNo4U: "a conservative target of this first internal low, this would leave you with a 6.6 RR, which is like typically what I would go for. I go for like everything from like 1 to 4 to 1 to 6."
- AGmRZ9Te9NY (London trade): "I just do my little one to three" — 1:3 acceptable when that's what structure offers.
- Management: BE after an internal high is taken (tNyT7tHOmGI: "when price took out this high, I moved my stop loss to break even. Cuz at that point... it would just be chop"); exit on SMT (4COROwkO3DI: "There is an SMT... ES is almost about to take out this high, which is not great for my trade... I'll probably just close my trade manually when it does").

## 5. Fib usage

- Y-oqSZmNo4U: "we have the 0.79 on the fib, which is like the most premium level on the fib" (mirror: most discounted for longs). tNyT7tHOmGI: "0.79 on the fib, it doesn't get more discounted than that. That's a bonus." [16:23]
- 0.62 = "the most... frequently used level on the fib. That's like beginning of OTE. Price rejects 0.62 to the tick a lot, which I would want to have my stop loss above this at least." (Y-oqSZmNo4U)
- Fib is confluence, not strategy: "The fib itself, in my opinion, not a strategy. It is a confluence and a very consistent one." (tNyT7tHOmGI, [2:24–2:29])
- Edging discount is tolerated: "as you can probably see, it edges the discount itself. Does that mean we can't take the trade? No." (tNyT7tHOmGI) — then scale down a TF for the entry trigger.
- Leg validity rule (Y-oqSZmNo4U): "these small legs are constantly getting rebalanced... this leg has already rebalanced to 50%... that's a new leg now, I can't use that fib in the same area anymore" — a fib leg dies once retraced 50%; re-anchor from the last unrebalanced extreme. Extend the fib top (bearish: bottom) on each new extreme after a fresh opposing close.
- Premium/discount discipline kills trades: a 5m RB "beyond the 0.79 on the fib... kind of too premium for me. So did not take that." (AGmRZ9Te9NY)
- Caveat: DeltaTrend's code-review video caught him anchoring the fib two different ways in the same (60k) video with no explanation — the anchor convention is genuinely underspecified at source.

## 6. Session timing & the 10 AM key-open retest

- 10 AM KO is the 4H open (Y-oqSZmNo4U): "The 10:00 a.m. is the 4-hour candle open... if you look at all these candles on the 4-hour time frame... They all have wicks on the top and on the bottom. Like 97% of them do at least. So... we can expect our 10:00 a.m. open to go in one direction and then for it to manipulate in the other direction."
- The KO-retest sequence in his own words (tNyT7tHOmGI): "10:00 a.m. opens lower, we distribute higher. Ideally for me, I want that retrace. I want the retrace to come down and retest 10:00 a.m. ... there is a bunch of confluences in this area telling me that yeah, this could be a good area to reverse." Confluences on that trade: 0.79 fib + 1m & 5m rejection blocks + 10 AM framework. [15:56–16:38]
- Judas framing (tNyT7tHOmGI): "What happens at open today? We Judas to the upside, Judas swing, fakeout, right? And then we dump down and we take out all these draws on liquidity... after we take them out, I'm going to tend to lean more bullish, especially if 10:00 a.m. opens lower... All we want with 10:00 a.m. is a lower wick... so that we can distribute to the upside." [14:04–15:15]
- Daily routine (Y-oqSZmNo4U): "I like to just go on the chart at 10 a.m. If there's news at 8:30, I'll trade like a data high low setup at 8:30 if it appears. If not, I'll look at 10 a.m. How is 10 a.m. opening, how is structure forming around this area... if I don't get what I want to see, I'll just simply get off the chart."
- No manipulation below the KO = no longs off it: "we close aggressively above 10 a.m., but we have no manipulation below it, which means I'm not going to long. Longs are out of the question." (Y-oqSZmNo4U)
- Midnight open: "95% of the time we will manipulate either below or above midnight open and then we will start the actual move" — near-zero manipulation below midnight makes it a *target*, not support.

## 7. Risk cadence & frequency

- tNyT7tHOmGI: "take max two losses a day, two to three trades per day, and stop... If you take three trades and they're all losers... you're offside. Just stop trading for the day." [10:27–10:47]
- Y-oqSZmNo4U: "I might take like three trades per week and they are just high quality trades... high RR and the win rate is pretty good because the trade frequency is so low. I'm not spamming high RR trades. That would leave me with like a 20% win rate."
- Loss tolerance is the design (tNyT7tHOmGI): a 12-pt RB loss followed by a 1:8 winner at the deeper 0.62 tap — "The thing about high risk reward is that we have room to fail... take the chances that are given to you."
- Failure mode on news days (WEeXKMzaJjY): sequences of 1m RBs get "absolutely ran through" around NFP; the fix is timeframe elevation — "see how we just cut through all that noise on the one minute time frame with one click [switching to 5m]" — and never countertrend into an unmet draw: "we're not trying to take bullish trades right before the draw on liquidity is met. That's stupid."
