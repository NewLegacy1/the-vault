# Stop Placement: Fixed vs Structure-Based vs Volatility-Scaled, Wick-Out Rates, and the Tight-vs-Wide Tradeoff

## Key findings

- **The only peer-reviewed anchor: stops add value if and only if returns are non-random-walk.** Kaminski & Lo ("When Do Stop-Loss Rules Stop Losses?", *Journal of Financial Markets* 2014) prove that under the random walk hypothesis simple stop rules *always reduce* expected return; under momentum or regime-switching processes the "stopping premium" turns positive, proportional to return persistence. Empirically on index futures, stop policies at longer sampling frequencies raised return and cut volatility. Caveat honestly: their setting is portfolio-level allocation at daily/monthly frequency — application to 1-minute trade stops is analogy, not direct evidence. Peer-reviewed.
- **Volatility-scaled (ATR) stops beat fixed-distance stops across regimes in every backtest surveyed.** A fixed distance is too tight in high volatility (constant wick-outs) and too loose in low volatility (oversized losses); ATR stops adapt automatically. One 9,433-trade multi-asset study: 2.0×ATR profit factor 1.26 vs 0.85 for fixed pips on the same signals (Quant Signals; StratBase; Panshi methodology). Vendor backtests, but unanimous in direction.
- **Consensus optimal band for intraday: 1.5–2.5× ATR of the entry timeframe.** Below ~1.5× the stop sits inside routine noise; above ~3× the loss size dilutes risk-adjusted return. An ES-specific grid found typical intraday noise ≈ 1.5×ATR and profit factor peaking at 2.0–2.25×ATR (win rate 45% at 1.5× → 58% at 2.0–2.25×) (QuantStrategy.io; StratBase multiplier table). Convention with converging backtest support; exact numbers vary by study.
- **Stop-out ("wick-out") rate falls steeply with stop width** — representative published tables: ~67% stop-out at 1.0×ATR, 52% at 1.5×, 41% at 2.0×, 29% at 3.0× (StratBase); a second source frames noise-only stop-outs as 28–35% at 1.0×, 15–20% at 1.5×, 8–12% at 2.0× (DXP Analytics). The two tables measure different things (all stop-outs vs noise-only) — treat the *shape*, not the levels, as the finding. Vendor data, no peer review.
- **The win-rate/RR tradeoff is pure geometry with no free lunch**: with zero edge, win rate ≈ stop / (stop + target); the market "will hand you any win rate you ask for" and price the payoff accordingly. A random-entry grid on NQ/ES reproduced this — the extreme corners hit 99% and 1% win rates with ~zero gross expectancy (tradingstats.net random-entry study). Break-even win rate = 1/(1+R). Any observed edge must be measured as distance *above* that line, not by win rate alone (journalplus; tradingstats). Data-backed and mechanically true.
- **Structure-based stops are thesis-invalidation stops but sit exactly where the liquidity is.** Swing lows/highs and pattern extremes hold queues of resting stop orders; participants with size push into those clusters (the "stop hunt" is a liquidity-seeking mechanic, not a conspiracy). The published mitigation: place the stop a *non-obvious* distance beyond the level — a buffer of ~0.3×ATR, or 0.3–0.5% of price, drops the wick rate "considerably" (retired.today; DXP Analytics; traderssecondbrain). Practitioner consensus; buffer sizes are opinion.
- **The professional synthesis is hybrid: structure sets the location, ATR sanity-checks the width, position size flexes to hold dollar risk constant.** If the structure requires a stop wider than ~2×ATR, skip the trade or find a tighter entry rather than eating oversized risk (DXP; retired.today "volatility-targeted" standard). Consensus practice.
- **Stop-parameter research hygiene**: optimize over a grid (e.g. 0.3–3.0×ATR), pick from a *stable plateau* of the Sharpe curve, never a single spike; healthy stop-trigger rates run ~30–60% of trades; >70% means whipsaw, <20% means stops are decorative (Panshi). Methodology guidance, consistent with wave-1 overfitting note.

## Details / mechanics

**ATR stop**: stop_distance = N × ATR(14) computed on the *entry timeframe* (1-min ATR for 1-min entries — using daily ATR for intraday stops is a listed classic error). Volatility-targeted sizing then sets contracts = risk_$ / (stop_pts × $/pt), decoupling placement from dollar risk.

**Structure stop with buffer**: stop = block/swing extreme ± max(k ticks, 0.3 × ATR(entry TF)). The buffer's job is to survive the liquidity sweep *through* the obvious level.

**Geometry identities** (no-edge baselines to measure against):
- Win rate baseline: WR₀ ≈ stop / (stop + target). For 1:5 (target = 5×stop): WR₀ ≈ 16.7%.
- Break-even: WR_be = 1 / (1 + R). Same 16.7% at R = 5.
- Expectancy in R: E = p(R) − (1−p) — evaluate stop variants on E, never on win rate or stop-out rate alone.

**Kaminski-Lo framework**: stopping premium Δμ = expected return with stop − without; Δμ < 0 under random walk (you sell after losses, and future returns don't care); Δμ > 0 ∝ serial correlation under momentum. Practical reading: a stop is a bet that *conditional on hitting it, continuation is more likely than reversion*. A stop placed inside the noise band is a bet on reversion continuing — usually the wrong side of that inequality.

**Stop-parameter grid protocol** (Panshi): sweep N over 0.3–3.0 in 0.1 steps on training data → require a flat performance plateau ≥ 3–4 neighboring values → validate the chosen plateau out-of-sample → check trigger rate lands in 30–60%.

## APPLICATION TO THE VAULT

- **The June ledger already looks like an ATR-multiple experiment run by accident.** Stops of 4.75–33.5 pts on the same instrument/timeframe are a 7× spread in noise multiples. The pattern — a 9.75-pt stop wicked before the trade ran to target, a 4.75-pt counter-bias stop fully lost, wins clustered at 11.5–33.5 pts — is exactly what the stop-out-vs-width tables predict if MNQ 1-minute noise around the 10:00 open is on the order of ~6–8 pts (so ~1.5× noise ≈ 9–12 pts, right where the winning cluster starts). That noise figure is a hypothesis, not a measurement: **log ATR(14, 1-min) at entry for every take from now on**, then compute each trade's stop/ATR ratio. Fifteen existing trades can be back-filled from replay data in one session.
- **Concrete diagnostic to accumulate**: scatter stop/ATR ratio vs outcome. If June's pattern holds — losses concentrating below ~1.5×ATR, wins above — the literature says the fix is *not* "wider stops everywhere" but a validity floor: a rejection block whose extreme sits closer than ~1.5×ATR(1-min) to the limit entry is a setup whose structure is smaller than noise; skip or wait for the next block. That's a one-parameter filter (50–100 trades to validate, per the sample-size note) and it doesn't touch the frozen entry/target logic.
- **The block-extreme stop is a structure stop at the most hunted location on the chart.** The stop sits "beyond the block extreme" — the exact liquidity pool the wick literature warns about. The published mitigation costs a few points: buffer = max(2–3 ticks, 0.3×ATR) beyond the extreme. On a 10-pt stop, +2 pts of buffer cuts the capped target from 50 to 50 pts (unchanged — target is points-based) and RR from 5.0 to ~4.2 — the Wilson-lower-bound expectancy math still clears break-even (break-even WR at 4.2R = 19.2% vs lower bound 41.7%). This is a *rule change* and therefore a Stage-0 question, not a live tweak — but it is the single most evidence-aligned candidate the stop literature offers for the wicked-out-then-ran failure mode.
- **The fixed 1:5-capped-100 geometry means win rate must be bought with stop width — check the price.** With target = min(5×stop, 100 pts), no-edge baseline WR ranges from ~16.7% (small stops) to ~25% (33.5-pt stop, where the cap binds at 100 pts → R ≈ 3). June's 67% observed vs ≤25% baseline is a large gap everywhere in the stop range — the edge is not a geometry artifact. But note the cap makes *wide-stop trades strictly worse RR* (33.5-pt stop → R = 3.0, break-even 25%): expectancy per trade at the point estimate is E = 0.67×3 − 0.33 ≈ +1.7R for capped trades vs +3.0R for uncapped — wide-stop takes need a genuinely higher win rate to pull equal weight. The June win-cluster at wide stops suggests they do; the tagged ledger will confirm or kill that within ~100 trades.
- **Per the position-sizing note (wave-1), stop width and size must flex together**: a 33.5-pt stop at fixed 10 MNQ is ~$670 risk vs $95 at 4.75 pts. The volatility-targeted standard — fixed dollar risk, contracts = risk/(stop×$2/pt/contract) — is the literature-consistent companion to any ATR-floor rule, with the wave-1 caveat that upsizing tight-stop trades is dangerous *precisely because* tight stops carry the higher wick-out hazard documented here. Resolve with stop-size-conditional win rates in the path Monte Carlo before changing sizing.
- **Kaminski-Lo sanity check on the whole design**: the system's stops are bets that a 1-minute close beyond the block extreme implies continuation against the position. That is a momentum assumption at the 1-minute scale — consistent with the variance-ratio/intraday-momentum evidence in the regime note (VR > 1 at short horizons near the open). The design is coherent; what's unverified is the noise floor, which the ATR logging fixes.
