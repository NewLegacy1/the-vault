# Break-Even Stop Moves and Trailing Stops: What the Backtests and Papers Actually Show

## Key findings
- **The most direct systematic test on record says BE-at-1R hurts, and 1R is the *worst* trigger.** Enlightened Stock Trading's long-horizon backtest (trend system, slippage and commissions included): adding move-to-BE-at-1R cut annual return 29%→~26%, *increased* max drawdown 28%→29.5%, dropped MAR 1.04→0.88, and cut WR 47.5%→45.9%. Sweeping the trigger from 0.25R to 5R: every trigger below 2R reduced returns, the minimum of the performance curve sat at exactly 1R (the most popular retail setting), and above 3R the BE move made no measurable difference either way. Data-backed, single system class (trend following on equities).
- **For mean-reversion/retracement entries specifically, the structural argument against early BE is stronger.** Mean reversion relies on adverse-then-favorable price paths; any stop tightening mid-path truncates exactly the trades that were about to work. Aligrithm's framework: a stop's two effects are loss-tail truncation (good) and winner-conversion (bad), and "for most mean-reversion strategies the second effect is larger" — tight stops (~1 ATR) typically drive the edge to zero (Aligrithm; Enlightened Stock Trading mean-reversion guide). A BE move is a mid-trade stop-tightening, so it inherits this math whenever price retests entry before running. Data-backed pattern across mean-reversion system tests.
- **Bulkowski's independent tests agree**: after scaling out, raising the stop to break-even "dramatically reduces profit... forces the system to exit trades that would otherwise be profitable" (thepatternsite). Data-backed.
- **The academic stop-loss literature is about a different question — but calibrates the tail-protection benefit.** Kaminski & Lo ("When Do Stop-Loss Rules Stop Losses?") show stops *subtract* expected return under a random walk and only add value when returns are serially correlated (momentum regimes); the SSRN trailing-stop study (Han et al. lineage) finds trailing stops have *inferior mean returns* to buy-and-hold but superior downside/risk-adjusted profiles, with the benefit concentrated in disaster names; Dolvin & Foltice (2023) find narrow trailing thresholds significantly *underperform* wider ones (best ~1.0–1.5σ). Translation to intraday: stops/BE-moves are insurance — they cost expectancy on average and pay in tails and trends. Peer-reviewed, on daily-horizon equities, so directionally instructive rather than directly transferable.
- **Where BE moves are defensible (practitioner consensus, not hard data)**: very high trade frequency where no single runner matters; imminent scheduled news; and prop-firm evaluation phases where the objective function is pass-probability, not expectancy — capping downside variance can rationally beat maximizing E when a trailing-drawdown barrier is close (botfxpro; multiple prop-trading sources). The same sources warn: whatever BE rule goes live must be the one that was backtested — "do not tighten up because the money is real."
- **Quantifying the "BE stop-out on noise" cost**: the KMF worked example shows the mechanism cleanly — if 40% of would-be winners retest entry after a +0.5R BE trigger, a 3R/40%-WR system's expectancy falls ~80%. The fraction of winners that retest entry (measurable from MAE data, see the MFE/MAE note) is the entire question; everything else is arithmetic. Illustrative math, not a universal constant.

## Details / mechanics
**Expectancy identity for a BE rule.** Let p = WR without BE, R = payoff, T = BE trigger level, f = fraction of eventual winners whose path touches +T then retraces to entry, g = fraction of eventual losers whose path touches +T first (these get "rescued" to 0 instead of −1R):
- E_noBE = pR − (1−p)
- E_BE = p(1−f)·R + p·f·0 + (1−p)(1−g)·(−1) + (1−p)·g·0
- ΔE = E_BE − E_noBE = (1−p)·g − p·f·R
The rule helps only when g/f > pR/(1−p) — at p=0.65, R=5 that threshold is g > 9.3·f. In words: **for a 65%/5R system, each winner sacrificed to a BE noise-out costs 9.3× what each rescued loser saves.** The rule needs losers to touch +1R nine times more often than winners retest entry — empirically backwards for retracement entries, where losers tend to fail fast and winners often re-test the wick.
- Same identity at R=3.33 (cap binding at 30-pt stop): threshold g > 6.2·f. Still lopsided.

**Trigger-sweep shape** (Enlightened Stock Trading): performance loss is worst at 1R, shrinks as the trigger rises, and vanishes above ~3R — because a BE move after +3R on a 5R target rarely gets touched before resolution. A "BE at 4R" rule is near-free and near-useless; a "BE at 1R" rule is expensive; there is no documented setting that *adds* expectancy.

**Trailing stops vs BE moves**: a trailing stop is a continuous version of the same trade-off and the academic results rhyme — tighter trails give up more mean return for more downside protection (Dolvin & Foltice: <1σ trails significantly worse). On a 5R capped intraday target the trail mostly competes with the fixed target and converts full winners into partial winners; it changes the payoff distribution shape the same way partials do (see MFE/MAE note).

**Tail-risk counterweight (the honest other side)**: the cost side above assumes losers are −1R. The real distribution has rare worse-than-stop outcomes (gaps through stops, halts, fat slippage) that BE/trailing rules genuinely truncate — Kaminski-Lo's momentum-regime value and the delisted-stock result in the SSRN study are the daily-horizon analogues. Intraday MNQ with a hard stop already caps most of this; the residual tail is slippage-through-stop on news spikes, which a BE move does not protect against anyway (it fills with the same slippage).

## APPLICATION TO THE VAULT
- **The frozen no-BE convention is not just a study convenience — it is the evidence-favored setting for this exact strategy class.** Retracement limit entries near the open are the adverse-then-favorable path type that BE moves damage most; the best documented sweep puts 1R (the natural candidate) at the exact minimum of the performance curve. Post-walk Stage-0 should treat "add Powell's BE move" as a claim requiring evidence, with the burden on the change.
- **The Stage-0 test is already fully specified by the ΔE identity: measure f and g from the May+June MFE/MAE ledger.** f = fraction of winners whose post-entry path touched +1R and later retraced to entry (would have been BE'd out); g = fraction of losers that touched +1R first (would have been rescued). Decision rule to pre-register now: adopt Powell's BE move only if g > 9.3·f in ≥60 combined trades (adjust the multiplier per-trade for cap-bound realized R). No new replay work is needed beyond the MFE/MAE logging already queued.
- **Prediction on record**: rejection-block winners frequently re-test the wick zone (that is the entry logic), so expect f in the 20–40% range and g near 10–15% — i.e., the BE move should fail the test decisively. At f=0.3, g=0.125: ΔE = 0.35×0.125 − 0.65×0.3×5 ≈ **−0.93R per trade**, roughly a third of the whole edge.
- **The one context where BE logic can re-enter**: eval-phase risk management on the prop account, where pass-probability near the trailing floor, not expectancy, is the objective. If the path MC (per the Monte Carlo note) shows BlowDD% concentrated in paths where an open 5R trade retraces from deep in profit, an *eval-only* protective rule can be considered — but it must be simulated in the path MC first, labeled as an account-rule overlay, and never back-propagated into the strategy's scorecard stats.
- **Powell's teachings vs Powell's context**: Powell's BE-and-partials style is coherent for a discretionary trader managing psychological drawdown and unbounded hold times; the frozen study is a fixed-target capped system where the same moves are measurably negative-sum. Both things can be true. Log this distinction in the Powell knowledge notes so the discipleship doesn't silently import the exit style.
