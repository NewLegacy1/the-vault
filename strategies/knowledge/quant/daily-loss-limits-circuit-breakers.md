# Daily Loss Limits, Circuit Breakers, and One-Trade-Per-Day Rules: The Tilt Evidence

## Key findings
- **The tilt effect is real, peer-reviewed, and found in professionals, not just retail.** Coval & Shumway-lineage work plus the Oxford "Do Losses Linger?" study of proprietary equity-desk traders: traders with morning losses take significantly more risk in the afternoon (chasing the mental account back to breakeven), and the traders most influenced by morning losses perform *far worse* — the behavior is consistent with prospect theory and the disposition effect (Shefrin & Statman 1985; Kahneman & Tversky 1979). Peer-reviewed, professional-trader dataset.
- **The break-even anchor is the specific mechanism.** A 707,152-transaction study of 5,379 UK spread traders (O'Sullivan et al., *Risk Analysis*) found strong effects of wealth changes *around the day's break-even point* on the decision to stop: traders below breakeven keep going (chasing); crossing back above it releases them. Historically profitable traders were less affected. This is the empirical basis for why "down on the day" is the dangerous state, independent of the dollar amount. Peer-reviewed broker dataset.
- **Trade-journal-aggregator numbers put a price on tilt** (large-n but non-peer-reviewed, methodology opaque — treat as directional): TradeMedic's 500k-account analysis found a measurable revenge-trading effect in ~37% of traders, costing an average ~$1,917/account and ~10% of total losses; JournalPlus-cited prop data shows retail WR dropping ~48%→32% on trades within 5 minutes of a loss; TradeZella reports tilt trades run 15–25 WR points below clean trades with profit factor frequently <0.5; recovery time to baseline averaged ~15 minutes (range 5–45). One aggregator's planned-vs-revenge table: WR 52%→34%, avg loser +51% larger, position size 1.4×, and a ~3–4× multiplier on the original loss across a revenge sequence. Numbers vary by source; the direction never does.
- **Funded accounts tilt harder than evals.** Hola Prime analysis of 15k+ trades from 96 traders: post-funding breakdown was driven by behavior more than strategy, with revenge trading *increasing* once funded and tilt appearing within ~1 minute of a losing funded trade (via Investing.com summary). Directly relevant to the upcoming live transition. Industry data, small-n, directional.
- **Overtrading has a six-decade paper trail**: Barber & Odean (2000), 66,465 households — the most active quintile earned 11.4%/yr vs 17.9% market; frequency is inversely related to net returns; overconfidence is the modeled driver. Barber et al. (2014, Taiwan): only ~5% of day traders profit; FGV Brazil futures study (19,646 day traders): 97% of those persisting 300+ days lost money. None of these test one-trade-per-day directly, but they establish that marginal trades beyond the best setups have negative expected contribution. Peer-reviewed.
- **Specific rule calibrations are practitioner convention, not measured optima**: daily loss limit at 1.5–2× your average losing day, or 40–60% of the account's max allowable drawdown; hard shutdown after 3 consecutive full stop-outs (reverse-engineered so the streak cannot pierce the daily limit); weekly tier: a day off after 3 consecutive negative days (NexusFi consecutive-loss protocols; Headge; Earn2Trade). No published study quantifies "win-and-done" — its rationale is entirely the tilt-avoidance evidence above (stopping while ahead removes the below-breakeven state from the rest of the session), plus the Barber-Odean marginal-trade-quality argument. Label it opinion-with-mechanism.
- **The consensus implementation finding: rules must be structural, not willpower.** Platform-enforced flatten-and-lockout beats a written note; the studies consistently show decision quality is worst exactly when the rule is needed (Headge; NexusFi; the Oxford paper's worst performers were the most loss-affected). Pre-commitment device logic, well-supported.

## Details / mechanics
**Why a one-setup strategy is structurally tilt-resistant.** Dual46 takes ~1 trade/day near the 10:00 open by design. The tilt literature's damage pathway is: loss → urge within minutes → *additional unplanned trade* at larger size with a wider stop. A frozen one-trade-per-day rule deletes the pathway: there is no second trade to take. The daily loss limit and the one-trade rule are the same rule here — the circuit breaker is the strategy definition itself.

**Calibration arithmetic for a rule set (NexusFi framework applied to Dual46 numbers):**
- Average losing trade ≈ 1R ≈ $95–$670 at 10 MNQ depending on stop width (per the Monte Carlo note's joint distribution).
- One trade/day → max daily loss = 1 full stop = worst case ~$670. A conventional "1.5–2× average losing day" limit is automatically satisfied; the binding constraint is instead the *prop firm's* daily loss limit — verify the widest structural stop (33.5 pts ≈ $670 at 10 MNQ) plus slippage cannot approach the firm's daily cap; if it can, size down on wide-structure days, which the stop-placement note already recommends for other reasons.
- Consecutive-loss tier: with one trade/day, "3 consecutive full stop-outs" = 3 consecutive losing *days*. Streak math note: at p=0.65 that has ~78% probability of appearing somewhere in 50 trades — it is normal operation, not a signal. So the correct Dual46 circuit breaker is NOT "stop after 3 losses" (that would fight the base rates); it is "never take an unplanned trade after a loss," which the freeze already encodes.
- The rule that actually needs writing: **a discretionary-sleeve lockout**. The realistic tilt failure mode is not breaking the script arm — it is the discretionary sleeve expanding on script-loss days. Rule: on any script-arm losing day, the discretionary sleeve is closed for the session. This is directly targeted at the Oxford/spread-trader finding (risk-taking rises after losses within the same session).

**Win-and-done in this structure**: with one planned trade, win-and-done is automatic. The evidence-based version of the idea for The Vault is the same sleeve-lockout in reverse — a script win does not license extra discretionary takes (the "house money" effect is the same mental-accounting bug as loss-chasing, per Thaler's mental-accounting lineage cited across these sources).

**Tilt telemetry worth journaling** (TradesViz/aggregator method, cheap to log): time-since-last-loss for every discretionary take; trades-per-day distribution on losing vs winning days; any size deviation from plan. Three numbers, and they make a future tilt problem visible in the data before it is visible in the account.

## APPLICATION TO THE VAULT
- **Going live is the high-risk moment, per the funded-account data.** Tilt appears within ~1 minute of a funded-account loss and is worse than in evals. Before the first live session, pre-register in the charter: (1) one script trade/day, no re-entry after a stop-out, ever; (2) script-loss day → discretionary sleeve closed; (3) platform-side daily loss lock set at the firm's limit minus one widest-stop trade, so a fat-fingered second trade physically cannot breach.
- **The June ledger already contains the audit trail**: check whether any June discretionary takes clustered on script-loss days or within 30 minutes of a script stop-out. If yes, that is the personal tilt signature — name it in the journal now, while it is cheap.
- **Do not adopt streak-based shutdowns for the script arm.** 2–3 losing days in a row is expected behavior at p=0.65 (see losing-streak note); a shutdown rule triggered by it would systematically remove the trades that follow normal streaks and add behavioral variance — same conclusion as the sample-size note's warning against streak-triggered unfreezing.
- **The rule hierarchy for the charter** (each tier's trigger is behavioral, not statistical): per-trade = the frozen stop; per-day = one script trade + firm's daily limit with a one-widest-stop buffer; per-session-after-loss = discretionary sleeve lockout; per-week = if the journal shows any unplanned trade taken within 30 min of a loss, one full day off and a written review before resuming. The last tier exists because the evidence says the violation, not the loss, is the leading indicator of account death.
- **Memorize the mechanism, not just the rule**: the danger state is *being below the day's breakeven anchor with time left to trade*. Every structural rule above works by making that state unactionable.
