# Rejection Blocks vs Order Blocks

## Key findings

- **Primary source for the rejection block exists and is transcribed:** ICT 2016 Premium Mentorship, Core Content Month 4, teaching 3.3 "ICT Rejection Block" (transcript on quagmyre.com XWiki). ICT frames it as a *distribution/accumulation pattern at swing extremes* — a turtle-soup-style liquidity raid read through candle **bodies**, not wicks alone.
- **ICT's canonical rejection-block anchoring is more specific than the community version.** Canon (Month 4 transcript): find the swing high with a wick or wicks, then frame the block between the **highest wick high** and the **highest open-or-close within the swing** — across *multiple candles* if multiple candles form the high, and regardless of whether the highest-body candle is bullish or bearish. Mirror for the bullish block (lowest wick low + lowest open-or-close). The community simplification ("zone = the sweep candle's body close to its wick extreme", ictkillzone, innercircletrader.net) collapses this to a single candle and usually gives a similar but not identical zone.
- **ICT explicitly says to treat the framed wick range "as a bearish/bullish order block"** (Month 4 transcript) — the rejection block is an order-block *variant* whose reference range is the wick area beyond the bodies, rather than a separate school of thought.
- **Canonical entry triggers (Month 4 transcript):** for a bearish RB, (a) aggressive: sell when price trades back up **to the low of the block range** — i.e., the highest open/close, where the wick *starts* — with a stop above; or (b) sell-on-stop *below* that level after price pokes above it without making a new wick high ("one of the few times I use selling on a stop"). Bullish mirror: buy "just below" the high of the block (the lowest open/close). The wick extreme is the outer boundary, not the entry.
- **Order block primary source:** ICT Core Content Month 4 "Orderblocks" (YouTube PIYh0CxoY9c; opentools summary). Canon: a bullish OB is the **lowest down-close candle with the largest open-to-close range near a support/institutional level**; validated when a later candle trades through its high. Entry at the **open of the down candle** (or a few pips above); the **mean threshold = 50% of the body (open to close, wicks excluded)**; the best OBs never see price trade below the mean threshold.
- **ICT's later-era OB refinement ties the OB to CISD** (change in the state of delivery): the OB is the run of consecutive opposing-close candles into the sweep, *validated* by a body close beyond the **opening price** of that run (precisiontradingrange "Order Blocks / CISD"; liquidityscan; innercircletrader.net CISD tutorial). Wick pokes don't count — body closes only. The popular "last down candle before the up move" is the early-era simplification.
- **Wick midpoints got a primary-source upgrade in 2024:** in 2024 Mentorship Lecture 8, ICT says **wicks should be treated the same way as gaps** — divisible into quarters with the consequent encroachment (midpoint) the most important level; price failing to trade past the lower half of a bottom wick is a sign of strength (ICT Sharks Lecture 8 notes). This retroactively legitimizes the community habit of marking a "rejection block mean threshold" at 50% of the wick.
- **No statistics.** ICT publishes no win rate for either block. The "55–65% win rate" figure on innercircletrader.net's RB guide is that author's own claimed experience, not measured ICT data.

## Details / mechanics

### Rejection block (ICT canon, Month 4)
1. Precondition: an established trend/bias — "ideal setups are found in major to intermediate term down trends" (bearish RB) and uptrends (bullish RB). The swept extreme should sit at/into a HTF level (old high/low, bearish OB, resistance).
2. A swing high forms **with long wick(s)**; price reaches **above the bodies** of the candle(s) forming the high, running buy-side liquidity, then rejects. A higher *high* is not required — the raid is on the highest **open/close** (failure-swing-by-bodies).
3. Frame the block: highest wick high ↔ highest open-or-close in the swing (any candle, any color). Bullish mirror at swing lows.
4. Trade it like an order block: bearish — sell at the block's lower edge (where the wick begins) with stop above; or sell-on-stop below that edge after a shallow poke above it that fails to make a new wick high. Bullish mirror.
5. Also valid as a **profit-taking reference**: cover shorts just below the lowest open/close of a prior swing low — "don't always demand that price gets below the wicks; it's really the bodies of the candle."

### Order block (ICT canon, Month 4 + CISD refinement)
1. Early canon: lowest down-close candle (largest body range) at a support/institutional level = bullish OB; validated when its high is traded through. Entry at the down candle's **open**; mean threshold = 50% of the **body**; a quality OB holds its mean threshold.
2. Later canon (CISD framing): the OB is the series of opposing-close candles into a liquidity sweep; it becomes tradable when a body close beyond that series' opening price confirms the change in the state of delivery. Displacement leaving an FVG upgrades the setup (community synthesis, consistent across sources).
3. Stops: community convention is beyond the full candle wick (never inside the wick zone); ICT's Month 4 emphasis is that the *mean threshold* holding is the quality test, so a body-close through the mean threshold is the earlier "setup degrading" signal.

### The actual differences
| Dimension | Order block | Rejection block |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reference range | Candle **body** (open↔close) of the last opposing candle(s) before displacement | **Wick** area beyond the bodies: highest/lowest open-or-close ↔ wick extreme |
| Requires liquidity sweep | Not in early canon (level + displacement); yes in CISD-era framing | Yes — the wick must run bodies/an extreme (turtle-soup DNA) |
| Entry (canon) | Open of the OB candle; mean threshold (50% body) preferred | Edge of the wick range (highest/lowest open-or-close); or stop-entry through it |
| Stop (canon/community) | Beyond the full candle wick | Beyond the wick extreme, small buffer |
| Sequence position | Before the impulse (accumulation footprint) | At/beyond the swept extreme (manipulation footprint) |
| Midpoint level | Mean threshold = 50% of body (ICT primary, Month 4) | Wick CE = 50% of wick (ICT primary only since 2024 L8; the "(wick+close)/2" formula is community codification) |

## APPLICATION TO THE VAULT

- **Your entry level is almost exactly ICT canon — more canonical than the community version.** You limit-in at the 1m rejection-block **wick-start**; ICT's Month 4 trigger for the aggressive entry is precisely "when price trades back up to the low of that range" — the highest open/close, i.e., where the wick begins. The community's preferred entry is *deeper* (wick CE, midpoint of the wick), which fills less often but gives a materially better price and tighter effective risk. Given your fixed 1:5 capped at 100 pts, an A/B tag in the journal — "would wick-CE have filled? Y/N" — costs nothing and directly measures whether the deeper entry would raise the realized R on the same setups.
- **One anchoring audit worth running:** canon frames the block with the **highest open-or-close of the whole swing**, across multiple candles if several form the high. If your 1m block marking uses only the sweep candle's own body, your wick-start level can sit several MNQ points away from the canonical edge whenever the swing high is built from 2–3 candles. Replay a handful of June fills and check which convention your hand-marked levels actually follow — consistency matters more than which one you pick, but you should know which one you're testing.
- **Stop beyond the block extreme = canon-consistent.** Both canon ("significant stop loss above that particular price level") and community (wick extreme + small buffer, 2–5 NQ pts) agree with your convention. The refinement from 2024 Lecture 8: a retrace that holds the **upper/lower half of the wick** (fails to cross wick CE) is ICT's sign-of-strength/weakness signal — a stop-out where price never body-closed past the wick CE is a distinct failure mode worth tagging separately before ever widening stops.
- **The sweep requirement is your quality filter.** Every primary and community source agrees a wick without a discrete swept pool (equal highs/lows, PDH/PDL, pre-market or overnight extreme, NWOG edge) is not a rejection block — it's just a wick. This is the same "swept level: Y/N + which" checklist tag the Judas note recommended; the two notes converge on it independently, which strengthens the case for making it mandatory in the Dual46 journal.
- **Timeframe nuance:** Month 4 was taught on daily charts, but ICT treats all PD arrays as fractal, and the community standard for NY-AM execution is exactly your habitat (1m blocks validated against HTF context inside a kill-zone/macro window). Your 1m usage is not off-canon — but canon *does* demand the HTF context (daily bias + meaningful swept level); a 1m rejection block with neither is the low-grade version ICT warns about.
